# INTERVIEW CHEAT SHEET: RETAIL DEMAND FORECASTING

================================================================================
1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH (30 Seconds)
================================================================================
"I built a production-grade retail forecasting pipeline using the Dominick's 
dataset, but the project's real value isn't just the code—it's the diagnostic. 
By running a forensic post-mortem on why my Naive baseline beat LightGBM, 
I identified a 'Promotional Saturation' regime where the exogenous signal 
collapsed, proving that data-generating conditions determine model success 
more than model complexity."

================================================================================
2. KEY TECHNICAL PILLARS
================================================================================
- DATA INTEGRITY: Audited 6M+ rows using 'OK' quality flags to prune noise.
- ECONOMETRICS: Used Fixed Effects (OLS) to isolate causal lift from baseline 
  store heterogeneity.
- MACHINE LEARNING: Benchmarked Prophet and LightGBM using a leakage-safe 
  12-week holdout.
- DIAGNOSIS: Identified Feature Degeneracy (95% promo intensity) in the holdout set.

================================================================================
3. TOUGH QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
================================================================================
Q: "Why did you use Fixed Effects instead of standard OLS?"
A: "Standard OLS leads to omitted variable bias. Stores in high-income neighborhoods 
   have higher baseline sales regardless of promos. FE 'absorbs' that store-level 
   noise so I can measure the PURE lift of the promotion."

Q: "Why did Naive beat LightGBM?"
A: "Feature Degeneracy. In the holdout period, promotions were active 95% of 
   the time. When a predictor becomes a constant, it loses its signal. In that 
   regime, the series is driven by autocorrelation, which the Naive model 
   specializes in."

Q: "What would you do next for the business?"
A: "I'd pivot to Store-Level forecasting. The chain-level aggregation hides 
   localized variance. Moving to the store level would reintroduce 'signal' 
   that ML models can actually learn from."
